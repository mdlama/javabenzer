Future Directions

There are a number of extensions that didn't make it into javaBenzer 7, but may be added in the future.

  • The spectre that overshadows everything else is automated problem solution. I (VS) developed the necessary algorithm back in the 1990's. However...
    • Having that available in a teaching tool does serious damage to its pedagogical value. If I can come up with a good solution to this issue, I'd love to make it available for use in a research setting, but don't expect to find it in javaBenzer any time soom.
    • Another thorny problem is what to do in the case of less than perfect data. With consistent data, producing a solution is trivial. However, if even a single interaction data point is "wrong", the algorithm may chase its own tail forever trying to produce a "perfect" solution.
    • Even if the data is perfect, solutions are not typically unique. Even beyond flipping the entire solution end-for-end, there can be smaller sections that could be ordered in different ways. How do I make it obvious that this is one of a number of possible solutions? For teaching this doesn't matter much, but it could be crucial in research.
  • Selecting (and moving) a group of rows (or columns) at the same time. There are a number of considerations for this item.
    1. For students, the problems are typically small enough that this doesn't provide significant value. During testing, I've solved problems with hundreds of items and cliques, and I acutely felt the need for that functionality.
    2. The display becomes a little confusing since the program can't determine if the user is selecting rows or columns until they perform a move or Order operation.
    3. If I did allow group selection, I'd also extend the Order operations (Sort/Reverse) to operate on selected groups, rather than the entire problem.
  • It would be nice (for large problems) to enable autoscrolling, so when you dragged the mouse out of the displayed area, the view scrolled. Again, not particularly valuable for small problems, but saves a lot of effort on large problems.
  • It would be nice to perform random problem generation for other types of problems than just deletion mapping (e.g. food webs). Unfortunately, most clustering problems have customized restrictions on what constitutes a valid problem, so each problem generator would need to be customized as well.
